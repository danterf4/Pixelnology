This week, Sony delivered some of the year's worst news: they are ceasing production of the discs used for physical game releases. This means we will lose the secondary market for used games, a segment that helped us counter the high cost of new titles.

We could simply skip buying games at launch and wait for prices to drop as people sold them off after getting bored or finishing the game. Losing this option deals a heavy blow to a strategy used by those trying to keep their hobby affordable.

On top of that, we have also lost the ability to lend games to one another. This practice was another great way to save money on the hobby; if you wanted to play a game that someone you knew already owned, you could borrow it instead of spending money on it.

Furthermore, GTA VI is set to launch as a digital-only title. Given the immense popularity and anticipation surrounding GTA, this move won't significantly hurt its sales, but it will likely encourage other companies to follow suit.

When Nintendo decided to release "KeyCards", cartridges that don't actually contain the game itself, but rather just a code allowing the Switch to download it, I was highly critical. It went against the grain of game preservation; the game isn't on the cartridge anymore, it's in the cloud, and the cartridge merely acts as a download key. However, other companies make such poor decisions by comparison that Nintendo's move doesn't seem quite so bad anymore.

Physical media is vital to the medium, not just for game preservation, but also because when you buy a physical copy, the game is yours, it’s your property, and no one can take it away. That isn't the case with digital games; platforms can decide to revoke access to a game you purchased without being obligated to refund your money.

Lately, I’ve been playing retro titles, specifically the Game Boy version of *Donkey Kong* this week. I’ve really enjoyed it; I was surprised to learn it’s essentially the first iteration of *Mario vs. Donkey Kong*. I hadn't realized that game had a "prequel," so to speak. It reminded me just how much the industry has changed and how trivial the issues games faced back then seem when compared to the problems we deal with today.

Or so I thought...

Then I realized that while all of this holds true for the AAA industry, it couldn't be further from the truth for the indie scene. We live in an era where indie games have frequently outsold AAA titles. The best and most entertaining concepts come from indie games; they dare to experiment with bold, risky ideas, which makes them all the more compelling. Plus, indie developers prioritize accessibility and affordable pricing, ensuring that all kinds of players can experience their games without barriers.

Another way gaming has become highly accessible is through retro consoles. I currently own a Retroid Pocket 5, a gift from my wife, and it’s one of the best consoles I’ve ever had. It handles emulation up to PS2, Wii, and Switch with ease; if you own the games, there are plenty of ways to extract the ROM from the cartridge or disc and play it on the device. You can also borrow games from friends. Or, if you’re feeling daring, you can sail the seven seas to find whatever game you want and load it up.

The Retroid Pocket 5 isn't a cheap option; it costs around $200. But hey, the PS5 costs around $500, and the price keeps climbing, as do the Xbox and Switch. Plus, there are more affordable alternatives, like the R36S, which costs about $30 and can run games up to the PS1 and N64 eras, and even many PSP titles.

The industry might not be in its best shape, but that’s only if we focus on what greedy corporations want us to do. If we look at the indie scene and preservation efforts, we’re actually in a golden age.

Let’s not let anyone tell us how to play or how to enjoy the medium. Let’s find ways to get the most out of the games and systems we already own. And if you don’t know what to play, I highly recommend *Donkey Kong* for the Game Boy. Many people might think it’s just a port of the arcade game, but keep playing, you’ll see that it has so much more to offer, despite being a Game Boy title.

Esta semana Sony nos dio una de las peores noticias del año, y eso es que van a dejar de producir discos para que los desarrolladores puedan sacar juegos físicos. Esto quiere decir que vamos a perder el mercado secundario de juegos usados. Esta parte del mercado nos ayudaba a pelear contra los juegos con precios muy altos.

Podíamos simplemente no comprar los juegos de salida, y esperar a que bajaran de precio cuando la gente los comenzaba a vender cuando se aburrían de ellos o los completaban. Lo que es un golpe duro a un truco para quienes intentaban no gastar mucho en el hobby.

Además de esto también perdimos la posibilidad de prestar juegos. Esto también ayudaba mucho a economizar el hobby. Si querías jugar algún juego, y alguien que conocías, ya lo tenía, podías pedirlo prestado y evitar gastar dinero en él.

Además, GTA VI va a salir de forma únicamente digital. Con la popularidad de GTA y lo mucho que el juego ha sido esperado, esto no va a afectar significativamente sus ventas. Esto va a incentivar a otras compañías sigan el mismo camino.

Cuando Nintendo tomó la decisión de sacar los KeyCards, que son cartuchos que no tienen el juego dentro, sino solo una clave que le permite al switch descargar el juego, lo critiqué mucho. Porque iba en contra de la preservación del juego, el juego ya no está en el cartucho, está en la nube, y el cartucho te permite descargarlo. Pero las otras compañías toman decisiones tan malas en comparación, que la decisión de Nintendo ya no suena tan mal.

El juego físico es importante para el medio por la preservación del videojuego, pero también porque al comprar un juego físico, el juego es tuyo, no te lo pueden quitar, es de tu propiedad. Pero con el juego digital no es así Las plataformas pueden tomar la decisión de quitarte tu juego, que compraste, y no están forzadas a devolverte tu dinero.
